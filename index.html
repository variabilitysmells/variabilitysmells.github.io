Investigating Variability-aware Smells in SPLs: An Exploratory Study

--SBES 2019 Supplementary Material--

Overview

Variability-aware smell (or variability-aware bad smell) is a bad smell definition for highly configurable systems (also known as Software Product Lines or just "SPL"), such as those implemented through feature-oriented programming and preprocessor variability mechanisms.

Variability-aware smell is a relative new research and increasing topic. However, this research topic has just an initial body of knowledge and there is very little evidence of smells related to the variability context. Therefore, the topic demands investigation to characterize variability-aware smells in SPLs and propose solutions to address them.

In our paper, we investigate evidence that variability-aware smells exist in SPL projects based on an empirical exploratory study. In the exploratory study, eleven open-source SPL projects provided data from diferent domains.

These studies are steps toward a better understanding of the context of variability-aware smells in SPLs and they contribute to the body of knowledge on variability-aware smells by (i) characterizing evidence that instances of variability-aware smell exist in open-source SPL projects, (ii) presenting evidence of associations between feature information, such as feature type and granularity, and number of variability-aware smell occurrences, and (iii) characterizing six new variability-aware smells. All these contributions provide insights to decrease the emergence of these smells in feature models and source code from SPL projects and consequently decreasing maintainability issues.

On this page, we provide supplementary material for that study.
