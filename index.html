Towards Variability-aware Smells: A Validation Study

--SPLC 2019 Supplementary Material--

Overview

Variability implementation adds a new dimension of complexity in highly-configurable system artifacts, such as source code and feature model, thus giving rise to new possibilities for design flaws. Bad smells are an established concept to describe design flaws or decay in software artifacts. However, existing smells have no notion of variability and thus do not support flaws regarding variable implementations.

Variability-aware smell is a relative new research and increasing topic. However, this research topic has just an initial body of knowledge and there is very little evidence of smells related to the variability implementation context. Therefore, the topic demands investigation to characterize variability-aware smells in SPLs and propose solutions to address them.

In this work, we validate a set of five new variability-aware smells for the context of software product lines feature model and source code artifacts. The last ones are variability-aware smells in the context of SPL projects implemented with #ifdef directives, annotation-based variability implementation (conditional compilation mechanism). These smells have been documented based on recurrent observations in open source SPL projects implemented with the Java language.

We aim at investigating whether the documented smells are in fact variability-aware smells and their impact on program comprehension, maintainability, and evolvability quality attributes. We intent to validate the documented variability-aware smell types based on an empirical validation study performed in three software companies. In this study, we reviewed instances of variability-aware smells reported by 16 software developers. Moreover, we investigated human factors that might influence on the detection of variability-aware smells. In particular, we investigated the human factors: experience in software development and the main programming language of the developers. We are interested in how these factors might influence on variability-aware smell detection.

The empirical validation experience reported and packaged in this paper can contribute to consolidate the variability-aware smells catalogue and discuss open issues related to variability implementation, maintenance and evolution. This work is one step toward to establish deeply knowledge on smell in the context of variability implementations by (i) characterizing evidence of five relatively new variability-aware smells; (ii) investigating how human factors can influence on variability-aware smell detection precision, effectiveness, and efficiency; and (iii) characterizing the impact of these smell on program comprehension, maintainability, and evolvability.
